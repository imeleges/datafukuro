Brief description

The project aimed to optimize production costs for a metallurgical plant by building a predictive model to forecast the temperature of steel. The primary metric for evaluating model performance was Mean Absolute Error (MAE), with a target value of no more than 6.8.

Description

The process of steel treatment happens in a metallic ladle with a capacity of approximately 100 tons. To ensure the ladle withstands high temperatures, it is internally lined with refractory bricks. Molten steel is poured into the ladle and heated to the desired temperature using graphite electrodes mounted on the ladle's lid.

The steel undergoes several processes, including desulfurization, chemical composition correction, sampling, and alloying. Before the first addition of alloying additives, the steel's temperature is measured, and its chemical analysis is performed.

This cycle repeats until the target chemical composition and optimal melting temperature are achieved. The molten steel is then dispatched for metal finishing or sent to a continuous casting machine. The final product emerges as slab billets.

After preprocessing, new features were explored, but multicollinearity led to some being discarded. Merging datasets introduced missing values, retaining 2,299 records.

Three models were assessed, with CatBoostRegressor proving most effective, achieving an MAE of 5.64. Visualization highlighted the significance of total power, initial temperature, and the wire_1 feed as crucial features.
